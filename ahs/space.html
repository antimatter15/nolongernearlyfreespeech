Project Mercury

Project Mercury was initiated in 1958, and completed in 1963 project mercury became the United States first man-in-space program. Objectives of the plan included:
  • To orbit a manned spacecraft around Earth
  • To investigate man's ability to function in space;
  • To recover both man and spacecraft safely.
    • This project became a success on May 5, 1961 with Alan B. Shepard Jr. becoming the first American in space. This was the beginning with the United States manned space program. Project mercury also included 19 other manned launches that laid the ground work for the project Gemini and later the Apollo moon-landing-program.

    Apollo

    Forty years ago the U.S. started their long journey beyond earth. On May 25, 1962 President John F Kennedy announced the goal of sending astronauts to the moon before the end of the decade this becoming Americas second manned space flight. After eight years of hard work on July 20, 1969 the commander of Apollo 11, Neil Armstrong, stepped out of the lunar module and took “one small step" in the Sea of Tranquility, calling it "a giant leap for mankind".

    The following years six other missions- Apollo 11, 12, 14, 15, 16, and 17, went on landing on the moon, studying soil mechanics, meteoroids, seismic, heat flow, lunar ranging, magnetic fields and solar wind. Apollo’s 7 and 9 tested spacecraft in Earth orbit; Apollo 10 orbited the moon as the dress rehearsal for the first landing.

    NASA’s need to work on the hostile, airless environment of space, during its Apollo missions and created many new technologies that are commonly used in too days space flights.

    Space Shuttle

    The first reusable rocket that could fly cargo and people on manned space missions to the space station. Even though the space shuttle manned space flight does not go far out into space it plays a vital role. These roles include sending satellites and other equipment into space and repair them there; it also is used to conduct science experiments in space that study human capability in space. This type of space travel program was currently closed by the U.S. on July 8, 2011 when space shuttle Atlantis was launched.
